Causes of Inflation

Inflation refers to the sustained increase in the general price level of goods and services in an economy over time. It reduces purchasing power, meaning that consumers can buy less with the same amount of money. Understanding the root causes of inflation helps policymakers, economists, and everyday people better prepare for and respond to economic shifts.

1. Demand-Pull Inflation

This occurs when aggregate demand outpaces aggregate supply. In simpler terms, if more people want to buy goods and services than what‚Äôs available, prices naturally rise. This is common during periods of strong economic growth when consumer confidence is high and spending increases rapidly.

2. Cost-Push Inflation

Cost-push inflation arises when the costs of production increase, forcing producers to raise prices to maintain profit margins. This can be driven by rising wages, increased costs of raw materials (like oil or metals), or supply chain disruptions.

3. Monetary Expansion

If a central bank increases the money supply too quickly, it can lead to inflation. More money in circulation chasing the same amount of goods causes prices to rise. This theory is often summarized by the phrase: ‚Äútoo much money chasing too few goods.‚Äù

Sometimes, just the expectation of future inflation can cause inflation. If businesses and workers expect prices to rise, they may increase prices or demand higher wages in advance ‚Äî which itself contributes to inflation. This creates a self-fulfilling cycle.
